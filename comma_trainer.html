Compound, Complex, Lists & Interrupters

Compound Sentences

A compound sentence connects two Main Clauses (complete thoughts) using a Coordinating Conjunction.

The Golden Rule

Use a comma before the conjunction.

Formula: [Main Clause] , [Conjunction] [Main Clause]

Remember: FANBOYS

ForAndNor ButOrYet So

"I wanted to buy the shoes, but I didn't have enough money."

Do NOT use a comma if the second part is just a verb (not a full Main Clause).

"I went to the store and bought milk."

Complex Sentences

A complex sentence contains one Main Clause and one Sub-clause.

üí° The Memory Trick

A Sub-ordinating Conjunction creates a Sub-clause.

Common Sub-ordinating Conjunctions (SWABIs)

SinceWhenAfter BecauseIfAlthough

Scenario A: The Opener

If the sentence starts with a Sub-clause, use a comma to separate it from the Main Clause.

"Although it was raining, we played soccer."

Scenario B: The Closer

If the Sub-clause comes after the Main Clause, you usually do NOT use a comma.

"We played soccer although it was raining."

Lists & Introductory Phrases

1. The Oxford Comma (Lists)

When listing three or more items, use commas to separate them. The comma before "and" is optional but highly recommended for clarity.

"I bought eggs, bacon, and toast."

2. The "Intro" Comma

If a sentence starts with a small phrase (not a full clause) that sets the scene, use a comma.

"In the beginning, the earth was void."
"Unfortunately, we lost the game."

Interrupters (The "Scoop" Rule)

Sometimes we interrupt a sentence to add extra, non-essential information. This is often called a Parenthetical Element.

üç¶ The Scoop Rule

If you can scoop the phrase out of the sentence and the sentence still makes sense, you must put commas on BOTH sides.

"My brother, who loves pizza, came to visit."

Check: "My brother came to visit." (Makes sense? Yes. So we need commas.)

‚ö†Ô∏è The Essential Trap

If you cannot scoop it out (because the sentence loses its meaning), do NOT use commas.

"The student who studies hard will pass."

(If we remove "who studies hard", we just have "The student will pass." Which student? We lost the meaning. No commas!)
